Building in public is uncomfortable. You ship something half-baked and someone on the internet tells you why it's wrong. You write about a decision and realize, mid-post, that you made the wrong call.

That's exactly why I'm doing it.

## Accountability Without a Manager

When I work on side projects, there's no sprint review, no code review, no one asking why a feature is late. Building in public creates a lightweight version of that accountability. The audience doesn't need to be large — a handful of people following along is enough to keep me moving.

## Learning Faster

Writing about what I build forces me to understand it. There's a version of learning where you convince yourself you understand something until you try to explain it. Public writing skips the self-deception.

## The Actual Work

Here's what "building in public" looks like for me in practice:

- Posting about what I'm working on, even when it's not ready
- Writing retrospectives when things don't go as planned
- Sharing what I learned, not just what I shipped

No growth hacking. No "5 lessons from building my SaaS" threads. Just documentation of what I'm actually doing.
